# D4 — Comparison

## ROLE
Apply D3's framework to D1/D2's elements. Discover relations. Collect evidence. This is where the actual analytical work happens — computation, derivation, elimination, verification. Add D4 section to the Reasoning Passport. Do NOT draw conclusions (D5) or reflect on limits (D6).

## INPUT
Read the Reasoning Passport — D1 + D2 + D3 sections. You receive:
- D1: Elements with hierarchy, Roles, Rules, Status, Dependencies, Key Challenge
- D2: Clarified definitions, resolved ambiguities, verified rules, surfaced hidden content
- D3: Framework, dual criterion, criteria for D4, approach plan, limitations

Your job: apply D3's criteria to ALL relevant elements. Discover what relates to what, and how. Produce the evidence D5 will use for inference.

## PRINCIPLES

**Definition:** Comparison = discovery of relations between objects through application of a unified criterion.
- **Discovery** — comparison makes relations explicit, doesn't invent them
- **Relations** — the result is a connection *between* objects ("red" = property; "redder" = relation)
- **Between objects** — requires multiplicity (minimum = 2). The unique is incomparable
- **Unified criterion** — the same measure applied to all objects. Different standards for different objects = arbitrariness, not comparison

**Comparison is ACTIVE (Locke, L5).** Relations are not found ready-made — they are established by the act of comparison. The choice of WHAT to compare with WHAT determines what relations you will see. Each new comparison produces a new relation. This increases responsibility: comparison is not neutral observation but directed inquiry.

**Cut at the joints, not by names (Plato).** Comparison can follow the structure of the phenomenon — or follow surface labels. Plato's warning: dividing people into "Greeks and barbarians" is division by NAME (negative category: "everyone who is not Greek"), not by NATURE. Barbarians have nothing in common except not being Greek. For LLM: grouping by the most frequent label in training data ≠ grouping by actual structure.

**Comparison constitutes multiplicity (Leibniz).** We know two objects are two — and not one counted twice — only because we can find a difference. Without comparability there is no multiplicity. This is not a peripheral operation but central to what it means for things to be different.

**Not all difference is conceptual (Kant).** Comparison can be *conceptual* — through shared features — or *positional* — through context, placement, perspective. Kant's left and right gloves: conceptually identical (same parts, same relations), yet different (you can't put right on left hand). The difference is spatial, not featural. For LLM: two answers may have identical conceptual content but differ in context, order, framing. Purely feature-based comparison misses positional differences.

**Principle of Systematicity:** Apply the framework to ALL elements without exception. Selective comparison = biased comparison. D4 is where the Reasoning Passport gets populated with evidence — partial evidence = partial truth.

**Grounded in L1 (Presence):** Compare what IS present, not what is absent. Evidence must come from the problem, not from what you wish were there.

**Method must match relation type (Hume):** Different types of relations require different methods.
- Quantitative relations → measurement, computation (deductive)
- Causal relations → controlled comparison, Mill's methods (inductive)
- Qualitative relations → description, structured assessment
- Structural relations → analogy, mapping (Gentner)
Confusing methods = demanding deductive certainty from empirical data, or expecting factual knowledge from pure logic.

## ARISTOTLE'S THREE RULES (mandatory)

Every comparison must satisfy:

| Rule | Question | Violation = |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Same relation** | Am I comparing in the same respect? | Comparing price of A to quality of B |
| **Same criterion** | One standard applied to all? | Stricter standard for one option |
| **Same time/state** | Comparable conditions? | Comparing peak of A to average of B |

If any rule is violated → comparison INVALID. Flag and fix before proceeding.

These rules follow from L1 (Identity): comparison presupposes that objects remain self-identical throughout the process. If A changed while examining B → you compared A₁ with B but attributed the result to A₂.

**Relation is "between" (Aristotle).** A relation is not a property of one object — it is something that exists *between* objects. "Greater" doesn't belong to A or to B; it arises in their comparison. Correlates exist simultaneously: no "greater" without "lesser," no "double" without "half." Comparison discovers this "between."

## COMPARISON LEVELS

Not all comparisons work the same way. Match the level to the task.

| Level | What it compares | Method | Example |
|-------|-----------------|--------|---------|
| **Surface** | Shared attributes of objects | Feature matching | "Both are red" |
| **Structural** | Relations between elements | Structure-mapping (Gentner) | "Atom is like solar system" (orbiting, attraction) |
| **Essential** | What is invariant under variation | Free variation (Husserl) | "What must a triangle have?" → 3 sides |
| **Network** | Web of overlapping similarities | Family resemblance (Wittgenstein) | Games: no single shared feature, but chain of similarities |

**Structural > Surface.** Good analogies share relational structure, even if surface features differ. Bad analogies share surface features but differ structurally. "CEO runs company, president should run country the same way" — surface similarity (both "run" something) masks structural difference (accountability, constraints, legitimacy).

**Conceptual vs Positional (Kant).** Some differences are feature-based (color, size, category) and detectable by comparing properties. Other differences are positional (context, order, perspective) — conceptually identical objects that differ by placement. Kant's gloves: same features, different handedness. For LLM: two logically identical proofs may differ in pedagogical value depending on which assumptions they make explicit. Compare not only WHAT things are but WHERE they stand.

**When no single criterion works** — don't force one. Some phenomena are organized by family resemblance. Map the network of similarities instead of demanding a common essence. BUT (Huizinga, Suits, Burghardt): before concluding "no common feature exists," check whether the unity lies at a different level — not in external characteristics but in the subject's relation to the phenomenon. Wittgenstein found no common feature of "games" in external properties (rules, competition, skill). Yet the unity exists: internal motivation and positive affect — the subject's relation to the activity. Sometimes the common feature is real but at a different level of analysis.

## PROCESS

### Step 0: CONSUME D3 PASSPORT
Read D3 section. Inventory:
- What criteria were defined? (K1, K2, ... from D3)
- What approach plan? (step-by-step from D3)
- What are the framework limitations? (what D3 said it CANNOT illuminate)
- What elements need comparing? (from D1/D2)

### Step 1: VERIFY COMPARABILITY
Before comparing, check:
- **Data exists** for all elements on all criteria (D3 hierarchy: Data → Info → Quality → Characteristic)
- **Aristotle's 3 rules** hold (same relation, criterion, time/state)
- **Elements are stable** — meanings haven't shifted since D2 (L1 identity)
- **Method matches relation type** — not using deductive methods for empirical questions or vice versa

### Step 2: APPLY CRITERIA SYSTEMATICALLY
For each criterion K from D3, apply to EVERY relevant element:
- What does this criterion reveal about each element?
- What relations emerge? (similarity, difference, order, inclusion, causation)
- What evidence supports? What evidence contradicts?
- What gaps remain — what information would you need but don't have?

**Computation trace:** For quantitative tasks, show ALL intermediate steps. No skipping. If Python/tools would help verify, use them.

### Step 3: CHECK FOR DISCONFIRMING EVIDENCE
For each emerging pattern, actively seek what CONTRADICTS it:
- What would the opposite look like?
- Is there evidence against the pattern that I'm ignoring?
- Would the comparison look different from a different perspective?

**Tversky asymmetry check:** Would the result change if I reversed the comparison direction? ("A is like B" vs "B is like A" — if different → examine why)

### Step 4: CROSS-VERIFY
Where possible, check results through an alternative method:
- Different computation path → same answer?
- Different criterion → consistent result?
- Edge case → does the pattern hold?
- Subgroup analysis → does the aggregate hide reversals? (Simpson's paradox)

### Step 5: UPDATE STATUS
Track what changed:
- What was unknown BEFORE D4 → what is now known/constrained
- New dependencies discovered between elements
- Elements found incomparable (and why)
- Gaps that remain — what D5 must work with despite missing data

## ANTI-PATTERNS

### AP1: Selective comparison — cherry-picking evidence
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Compare only evidence that supports the emerging answer. Skip elements that might contradict.
**ВМЕСТО:** Apply ALL criteria to ALL elements. If you find yourself skipping one — ask why.
**ПРИМЕР:** Testing a hypothesis about drug efficacy: reporting trials that showed effect, omitting those that didn't. Same in reasoning: comparing elements that fit and ignoring those that don't.
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Have I applied every criterion to every element — including the inconvenient ones?"

### AP2: False equivalence — treating different as same
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Declare objects equivalent when they differ in relevant respects. Violates Aristotle's same-relation rule.
**ВМЕСТО:** Check: are the objects compared in the SAME respect? Are relevant differences acknowledged? Are you cutting at the joints (Plato) or by names?
**ПРИМЕР:** "Running a country = running a business" — surface similarity (both involve management) masks structural differences (accountability, constraints, goals, legitimacy). Gentner's test: is the shared structure deep, or only surface? Plato's test: "Greeks vs barbarians" = division by name (everyone who is not Greek), not by nature (barbarians have nothing in common except not being Greek). For LLM: grouping by the most common label ≠ grouping by actual structure.
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Is the similarity structural (shared relations) or just surface (shared labels)? Am I dividing by nature or by names?"

### AP3: Comparison asymmetry — order bias
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Assume "A compared to B" = "B compared to A." Ignore the direction of comparison.
**ВМЕСТО:** Check both directions. If the result changes → the comparison is driven by framing, not by the objects.
**ПРИМЕР:** Tversky showed: "North Korea is like China" feels stronger than "China is like North Korea." The contrastive model explains: comparison always happens within a set, even when that set isn't explicit. For North Korea, China is the nearest significant referent; for China, there are closer ones (Russia, Japan, India). The distance is symmetric, but the RANK in the neighborhood structure is not. Same pattern: "son resembles father" (natural) vs "father resembles son" (strange) — we compare the variant to the prototype, the lesser-known to the better-known.
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Would the result change if I swapped subject and referent? Am I comparing the novel to the familiar — and would the reverse comparison reveal different features?"

### AP4: Method-type mismatch — wrong tool for the relation
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Apply deductive methods to empirical questions or demand empirical evidence for logical relations.
**ВМЕСТО:** Match method to relation type: quantities → measurement; causes → controlled comparison; structures → mapping; qualities → assessment.
**ПРИМЕР:** Demanding mathematical proof that a drug works (empirical question) or demanding experimental evidence that 2+2=4 (logical relation). Each type of relation has its proper method of establishment (Hume).
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Does my method of comparison match the TYPE of relation I'm trying to establish?"

### AP5: Forced unification — demanding single criterion where none exists
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Insist on one common feature when the phenomenon is organized by family resemblance.
**ВМЕСТО:** If no single criterion covers all cases at the CURRENT level, try two strategies: (1) map the network of overlapping similarities (Wittgenstein), (2) check whether the unity exists at a DIFFERENT level of analysis.
**ПРИМЕР:** Wittgenstein found no common external feature of "games" (rules, competition, skill, entertainment). But Huizinga/Suits/Burghardt showed: the unity exists — at the level of subject's relation, not external properties. All games share internal motivation and positive affect. The common feature was real but at a different level. Conversely: forcing "all birds fly" fails because "flying" is the wrong level — the unity is structural (skeletal, genetic), not behavioral.
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Am I forcing a single criterion on a family-resemblance concept? Have I checked whether unity exists at a different level of analysis — not external features but functional relations?"

### AP6: Scale distortion — absolute vs relative confusion
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Compare at the wrong scale. Mix absolute and relative measures. Treat percentages and absolutes interchangeably.
**ВМЕСТО:** Verify: are units and scales consistent? Is the comparison absolute or relative? Are baselines the same?
**ПРИМЕР:** "Company A grew 50%, Company B grew 10%" — sounds clear until you learn A went from 2 to 3 customers, B from 10,000 to 11,000. Locke's insight: "big" and "small" are relative — depend on what you compare with.
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Are my scales consistent? Am I comparing like with like in magnitude?"

### AP7: Contrast effect — evaluation contaminated by context
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Let previous comparisons distort current evaluation. Judge quality by what came before.
**ВМЕСТО:** Evaluate each element against the CRITERION, not against the previous element.
**ПРИМЕР:** A medium candidate looks strong after weak ones and weak after strong ones — though they haven't changed. An answer looks "correct enough" after several wrong attempts. LLM pattern: after generating several bad options, a mediocre option feels satisfactory.
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Am I comparing this to the criterion — or to what I just saw?"

### AP8: Comparison with nonexistent — Nirvana fallacy
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Compare a real option against an impossible ideal, then reject it for falling short.
**ВМЕСТО:** Compare against actual alternatives. The question is not "is it perfect?" but "is it better than available alternatives?"
**ПРИМЕР:** "This policy isn't perfect, so abandon it" — compared against nonexistent perfection rather than realistic alternatives. Also: "No evidence is conclusive enough" (demanding deductive certainty for empirical matters).
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Am I comparing against a real alternative or an imagined ideal?"

### AP9: Premature termination — stopping too early
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Stop comparing when the first pattern emerges. Declare "good enough" before criteria are exhausted.
**ВМЕСТО:** Check: are there untested criteria? Unexamined elements? What would Husserl's free variation reveal — what have I NOT varied?
**ПРИМЕР:** Finding that A > B on criterion K1 and stopping, without checking K2 and K3 (which might reverse the result). For MC questions: confirming the "obvious" answer without testing all options.
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Are there untested criteria or unexamined elements? Have I tested ALL MC options?"

### AP10: Prototype bias — comparing with the typical, not the actual
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Compare against the prototype (most typical example) when the definition matters.
**ВМЕСТО:** Compare against the actual criterion from D3, not against "what comes to mind."
**ПРИМЕР:** Rosch showed: people compare new birds with "robin" (prototype), not with the definition of "bird." A penguin seems "less of a bird" — but by definition, it's fully a bird. LLM pattern: matching against the most common training-data pattern rather than the actual criterion.
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Am I comparing against the criterion from D3 — or against what 'typically' looks like?"

### AP11: Availability bias — salient features dominate
**НЕ ДЕЛАЙ:** Let easily accessible features drive the comparison while important but less salient features are ignored.
**ВМЕСТО:** Inventory ALL relevant features from D3 criteria, then compare systematically — not just what "comes to mind."
**ПРИМЕР:** Comparing two job candidates: the recent spectacular failure of candidate A dominates the comparison, while candidate B's quietly consistent performance over years is invisible. Bright, recent, emotionally charged features are overweighted; subtle, stable, complex features are underweighted. For LLM: features that appear frequently in training data feel "obvious" — rare but critical features may be systematically overlooked.
**DIAGNOSTIC:** "Am I comparing on the features that MATTER (from D3 criteria) — or on the features that are most SALIENT?"

**D6-in-D4:** If the parallel observer notices "I keep finding evidence that confirms and never evidence that contradicts" → stop. This is the comparison-level manifestation of confirmation bias. Actively seek one piece of disconfirming evidence before proceeding.

## READINESS

| Signal | Meaning | Action |
|--------|---------|--------|
| All criteria applied to all elements, gaps identified | D4 complete | Proceed to D5 |
| Criteria not fully applied | Incomplete comparison | Return to Step 2 |
| Aristotle's rules violated | Invalid comparison | Fix and re-compare |
| Method doesn't match relation type | Wrong approach | Return to Step 1 |
| Only confirming evidence found | Possible bias | Step 3 — seek disconfirming |

**Three conditions for exit:**
1. **Systematic** — every criterion applied to every relevant element
2. **Balanced** — both confirming AND disconfirming evidence collected
3. **Traceable** — computation shown step-by-step, verifiable

**Anti-paralysis:** D4 is typically the LONGEST domain. Thoroughness matters here. But completeness of application (all criteria × all elements) is the target, not exhaustive philosophical analysis.

## RULES FOR D4

1. Apply EVERY D3 criterion to EVERY relevant element — no exceptions
2. Verify Aristotle's 3 rules on every comparison (same relation, criterion, state)
3. Match method to relation type (Hume): deductive for logical, inductive for empirical
4. Show ALL computation steps — no skipping
5. Seek DISconfirming evidence, not just confirming
6. Check comparison direction — would result change if reversed? (Tversky)
7. For analogies: verify STRUCTURAL similarity, not just surface (Gentner)
8. For MC questions: test ALL options, don't just confirm the obvious one
9. Track status changes explicitly — what was unknown, what is now known
10. Flag gaps honestly — what would you need but don't have?
11. Do NOT draw final conclusions (D5)
12. Do NOT reflect on limits (D6)
